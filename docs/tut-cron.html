Scheduled tasks (cron)

Ask Whisper Studio to run a prompt on a schedule and its results come back to you inside the same conversation. No dashboard to check, no calendar reminder to ignore, and no hit to your token bill.

A cron job is just a prompt plus a schedule. You describe both in plain language, Claude sets it up with the cron_create tool, and from then on the job fires on its own. Every run posts a small card into the chat that created it, so the whole history reads top to bottom like any other conversation. This tutorial covers creating a job, reading its result cards, managing jobs from the panel, and stopping them.

Create a job in plain language

You do not fill in a form to start. Just say what you want and how often, right in the composer. Claude picks the right schedule shape and calls cron_create for you.

Every 10 minutes, check our SageMaker endpoints and flag any that aren't InService.

A small pill appears inline confirming the job was scheduled, something like Cron sagemaker-check scheduled · every 10 min. That pill is the cron_created event, and it stays in the transcript as a marker of when the job began.

  1. Describe the task and the cadence

    Say the instruction and the timing together, for example every 30 minutes, daily at 9am, weekdays at noon, or once tomorrow at 08:00. Claude maps your words onto a schedule (see Schedule types below).

  2. Claude calls cron_create

    Behind the scenes the model invokes the cron_create tool with a name, the prompt to run, and a schedule. The job is written to disk so it survives a restart, and the scheduler arms it immediately.

  3. The scheduled pill appears

    You get an inline confirmation with the job name and a human-readable schedule label. Nothing else happens until the first trigger comes due.

Give the job a memorable name when you ask ("call it sagemaker-check"). Names must be unique, and you will use the name later to edit, run, or delete the job.

Results land back in the chat

When a job fires, its prompt runs and the output posts as a result card in the conversation that created the job. The card header tells you at a glance what happened, and the body renders the run's output as Markdown so it is readable without clicking anything.

A finished run shows:

  • The job name and whether it fired or failed.
  • A status chip, ✓ ok or ⚠ failed, plus how long the run took (for example 820ms or 8.2s).
  • The full run output, rendered inline.
  • A small Next run line at the bottom so you know when it will fire again.

The most useful part: a result card appears in its originating conversation even if you are looking at a different session at the time. You do not have to have that chat open, and you do not have to be watching. Come back to the conversation later and every past run is still there, in chronological order, interleaved with the rest of the thread.

If you delete or archive the conversation a job belonged to, its future runs are not lost. They redirect to a pinned Scheduled Reports inbox session, so a job never fires into a void.

Cron cards do not cost you tokens

Cron result cards are UI-only. They render in the transcript, but they never enter Claude's prompt on your next message. The backend filters cron event rows out of the visible history before it builds a turn, so a busy schedule with dozens of past runs adds nothing to your context window and nothing to your token bill.

In practice that means you can leave a chatty monitoring job running for weeks without it slowly inflating the cost of every reply you send in the same conversation. The runs themselves get the same tool pool an interactive turn gets; what stays flat is the cost of your own turns. When a single run produces a lot of output, the card keeps it readable rather than sprawling.

Schedule types

Every job has one of three schedule shapes. You express them in plain language and Claude picks the right one; the table shows what each maps to and when it fires.

TypeFiresYou might say
intervalEvery N minutes, anchored to when it was created.every 10 minutes, every 2 hours
cronAt a wall-clock time, timezone-aware and correct across DST.daily at 9am, weekdays at noon, every Monday 8am
atOnce, at a specific instant, then it disables itself.tomorrow at 08:00, on 2026-07-06 at 9am

Wall-clock schedules use your host system timezone by default, so daily at 09:00 fires at 9am your local time and stays correct when the clocks change. You can pin a different timezone by naming one (an IANA name like America/New_York). Wall-clock times also accept several values at once, so at 9 and 4 becomes two firings a day, and a day-of-week can be a single day (mon), a range (mon-fri), or a list (mon,thu). For how these triggers are built and stored, see Cron / scheduled tasks.

Manage jobs in the Scheduled Tasks panel

For a bird's-eye view of everything you have scheduled, open the Scheduled Tasks panel. It lists every job with its schedule label and next run, and it is where you go to inspect and tweak jobs without going through chat.

Schedule builder

Create or edit a job's cadence directly, choosing interval, wall-clock, or one-shot. A live preview shows the resulting label and the next fire time before you save.

Run history

Each job keeps a capped history of its recent runs with status and duration, so you can see whether the last few checks passed or failed at a glance.

Unread badges

New runs surface as unread badges in the sidebar, drawn from the most recent runs across all jobs, so a fresh result gets your attention even when you are elsewhere.

Enable, run, delete

Pause a job without deleting it, fire one off-schedule right now to test it, or remove it for good. A paused job keeps its history and can be re-enabled later.

Editing and stopping jobs

You can manage jobs from chat too, in the same plain language you used to create them. Claude has a small set of cron tools it maps your requests onto:

ToolWhat it does
cron_createSchedule a new recurring or one-time job.
cron_listList every job with its schedule and next run.
cron_updateChange a job's prompt, schedule, name, or enabled state.
cron_runRun a job once right now, off its schedule.
cron_deleteDelete a job by name.

So you might say "list my cron jobs", "run cron sagemaker-check now", or "change sagemaker-check to every 30 minutes". Stopping a job is one sentence:

delete cron sagemaker-check

A small Cron sagemaker-check removed pill confirms it, and no further runs will fire. Editing a schedule re-anchors the cadence to now, so the new timing does not fire off a stale last-run.

A cron run gets the full chat tool pool: web search, tasks, sub-agents, skills, AWS, MCP and the browser, and, when a workspace folder is open, the workspace and Git tools too. Only ask_user_question is removed, because an unattended run has nobody to answer it, and any tool that would pause for your approval stops the run with a "[stopped] a tool requested user input" message rather than hanging. Write prompts that can finish on their own, without a mid-run confirmation.

What makes a good cron job

Cron shines for anything you would otherwise check by hand on a rhythm. A few examples:

  • A periodic health check, such as flagging AWS endpoints that are not in service.
  • A morning digest that summarizes something and posts it at 9am on weekdays.
  • A one-shot reminder that runs a prompt once at a specific time and then disables itself.

Because the result lands in the conversation that asked for it, a job doubles as a running log: the request, the schedule, and every result read together as one thread you can scroll back through.
