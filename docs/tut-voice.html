Voice & meetings

Two ways to put your voice to work: dictate messages straight into the chat box, or record a meeting and get a live, speaker-labelled transcript you can turn into a summary. Everything is transcribed on your own machine.

Whisper Studio has two independent voice features. Dictation types your speech into the chat composer (and can even submit it hands-free). Meeting recording captures a whole conversation into a separate transcript panel with speaker labels. They can run at the same time, because both share one microphone under the hood.

Dictate a message

The microphone icon sits between Attach and Send in the chat input. Click it and start talking: the chat textarea auto-focuses and transcripts append as they arrive, word by word for the sentence in progress and then committed as each sentence settles. Click the icon again (or press it while it is active) to stop.

Submit hands-free. End a sentence with a spoken submit command like okay send, send now, submit now, fire away, or send the message and it is sent for you. The command words are stripped from the body first, so "summarize the meeting, send now" is sent as "summarize the meeting". A bare verb on its own ("send") never fires, so a one-word mishear cannot submit by accident.

Dictation is tuned for low latency rather than long recordings. It always uses the streaming Parakeet engine and short half-second audio chunks, so trailing words (and your spoken submit command) reach the server quickly. It also runs without speaker identification, which is what lets it operate alongside a meeting recording without the two fighting over the same speaker profiles.

Record a meeting

The Record button lives at the top right of the window. Click it to start capturing your default microphone; a live transcript with speaker labels grows in the Transcript panel on the right. Click Record again to stop. On your very first recording the transcription model downloads automatically; there is nothing to configure first.

  1. Click Record

    Grant the microphone permission if the browser asks. The transcript panel opens and starts filling in as people speak.

  2. Watch it transcribe live

    Finalized sentences stack up as segments; a lighter "live" row shows the sentence currently being spoken and is replaced the instant it settles. The panel auto-scrolls to the newest line.

  3. Stop when the call ends

    Click Record again. Whisper Studio waits a beat for any in-flight sentence to land, commits it, and saves the transcript to the session that owns the recording.

Recording binds to the session it started in. You can switch to a different session and keep talking; the header shows a chip to jump back to the one that is recording, and the transcript saves to that session no matter what you are looking at.

Recording a call? To also transcribe what the other people say, add a Chrome tab or your system audio as a second source with the headphones button next to Record. See Capturing system audio below.

Choosing an engine and speaker count

The transcript panel header has two dropdowns that change how transcription behaves, and both can be changed live, even mid-recording:

ControlOptionsWhat it does
EngineWhisper / Parakeet (live)Whisper transcribes whole utterances at pauses (accurate, slightly delayed). Parakeet streams a growing draft word by word (lower latency).
Speakersauto or 1–8A hint for diarization. Auto estimates the count from the audio; telling it the real number makes speaker labels far more accurate, because clustering cuts at exactly that many speakers.

Speaker labels and renaming

Speaker identification happens locally. Each segment is tagged Speaker 1, Speaker 2, and so on, and consecutive lines from the same speaker are merged into one growing segment. As the recording continues, diarization can re-cluster and correct labels it assigned earlier.

Click a speaker label to rename it. Type a real name (Maya, Bob) and it applies everywhere that speaker appears; the rename persists for the session, so every past and future line from that speaker shows the friendly name.

Every transcript line is interactive. Select any text in the panel and right-click to copy it (with or without a speaker prefix), quote it in the chat input, edit the segment, search your workspace for the phrase, or have Claude explain, translate, summarize, define terms, or suggest follow-up questions about it.

One microphone, both features

The chat-input mic and the Record button share a single reference-counted MediaStream and AudioContext. When one feature is already recording, the other reuses that same device instead of opening a second getUserMedia stream (which the OS often resolves with silence when the device is already in use). The underlying microphone is only released when the last consumer lets go. In practice this means you can dictate a note into chat while a meeting is still being recorded, and neither one interrupts the other.

It stays on your laptop

Audio is captured in the page, streamed over a WebSocket to the local server on the same origin, and transcribed by an on-device engine. None of it goes to the cloud. Claude is only involved when you deliberately send a chat message, and even then Claude receives the text transcript, never the audio. See Transcription & diarization for the full pipeline.

Turn a recording into a summary

Once you have a transcript, ask Claude about it in chat. Because the transcript already lives in the session, a plain-English request is enough:

Summarize the discussion and list action items per speaker.

Beyond free-form questions, Whisper Studio has three ready-made ways to summarise a call. You do not pick them from a menu; just ask in plain English and the right one runs:

Ask forWhat you get
A quick summaryA fast pass over the transcript in one of four styles: action items, meeting notes, key points, or a short brief. Just say which one you want, for example "give me the key points" or "list the action items".
A full write-upA polished set of meeting notes: an overview, the decisions made, an owner / action / deadline table, the main discussion points, any blockers, and the next steps. Ask for "proper meeting notes" or "a full write-up of the call".
A catch-upA plain-language recap for someone who joined late, followed by a few suggested follow-up and clarifying questions to ask. Try "catch me up on this meeting".

Long meetings just work. A multi-hour recording can be longer than the model can read in one pass. You do not need to do anything special: the app condenses the transcript automatically before it summarises, so nothing is silently dropped. See how condensation works for the details.

Other prompts that work well: "What did we decide about the launch date?", "Draft a follow-up email to the team", or "List every open question raised in this call". When you are happy with the result, use /export to download the chat (including the summary) as Markdown. See Your first chat for more on prompting and export.

The Transcript panel also has its own Export button, which saves just the raw transcript (each line prefixed with its speaker) as a text file. Use that when you want the words without the chat conversation around them.

Capturing system audio (Zoom, Meet, and calls)

The Record button captures your default microphone. That records your side of a call and anything your mic picks up from the room, but not the audio the other participants send to your speakers. There are two ways to add that other side. The quickest is built into the app: add a Chrome tab as a second source, with no system setup. For a system-wide capture that covers every app and browser at once, route mic and system audio into one virtual input device with BlackHole.

In the app: add a Chrome tab (no setup)

Whisper Studio can capture the audio of a single browser tab and mix it into the same stream as your microphone, so both your voice and the call land in one transcript. It works in Chrome and Edge. Wear headphones so the tab's sound is not picked up a second time by your mic.

  1. Open Audio sources

    Click the headphones button next to Record. The picker lists your microphone, which is always on, and Chrome tab audio.

  2. Choose a tab

    Click Choose tab, pick the tab playing the call or video, and turn on Also share tab audio in the browser dialog before you share. If you skip that checkbox nothing is captured and the app tells you to choose the tab again.

  3. Record as usual

    Click Record. The header shows Mic + Tab, both sources are mixed into one stream, and the usual speaker labels separate the voices. Clicking Stop, or Chrome's Stop sharing bar, ends the tab capture.

Chrome and Edge only. Safari and Firefox cannot hand a tab's audio to a web app, and the embedded in-app preview cannot either, so open Whisper Studio in a normal Chrome or Edge tab. The tab source is armed per recording, so add it again for the next one.

System-wide: a virtual input device (BlackHole)

To transcribe both sides of any app or browser at once, route mic and system audio into one virtual input device using BlackHole and macOS Audio MIDI Setup. This is a one-time setup; after it, every browser and every recording just works.

  1. Install BlackHole 2ch

    brew install blackhole-2ch

  2. Create an Aggregate input device

    In Audio MIDI Setup, click + and choose Create Aggregate Device. Tick your Built-in Microphone and BlackHole 2ch. Keep the built-in mic at the top as the clock source, enable drift correction on the others, and name it something like Mic + System.

  3. Create a Multi-Output device

    In the same panel, click + and choose Create Multi-Output Device. Tick Built-in Output (or your headphones) and BlackHole 2ch. Keep the built-in output at the top as the clock source and name it Speakers + Loopback. This lets you still hear the call while its audio is also copied into BlackHole.

  4. Route it in System Settings

    Under System Settings → Sound, set Output to your Multi-Output device and Input to your Aggregate device.

  5. Refresh and record

    Reload Whisper Studio, click Record, and grant the mic permission once. Both your voice and anything macOS plays are now transcribed together.

Caveats. A Multi-Output device's volume cannot be adjusted from the menu bar, so use per-app volume instead. Bluetooth headsets often run at a lower sample rate, so avoid using one as the clock source. For the full walk-through and the latest details, see the Capturing system audio (macOS) section of the project README.
